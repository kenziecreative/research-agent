# Customer Safari

**Finding Tags:**
- **PAIN** — A struggle people express, explicitly or implicitly. Includes direct statements ("I need X"), emotional expressions ("I'm so frustrated"), behavioral patterns (asking the same question weekly), and workarounds (duct-taping multiple tools together). Sub-classify every pain as 🔥 Painkiller (emotional intensity, financial urgency, active solution-seeking) or 💊 Vitamin (aspirational, nice-to-have, low urgency). Trust behavior over stated severity — someone casually mentioning a "small annoyance" who posts about it 12 times in 3 weeks has a painkiller problem.
- **DELIGHT** — Genuine satisfaction or enthusiasm about something that works. Includes expressions of gratitude, excitement about new possibilities, unexpected benefits ("not only did this help my X, it gave me confidence to do Y"), and loyalty statements ("I've tried others but I always come back"). Reveals what actually works vs. what people merely tolerate.
- **RECOMMENDATION** — How people naturally suggest solutions to each other. Three types: advice-centered ("do this, try this"), reference-centered ("read this, listen to this person"), and purchase-centered ("buy this, don't waste money on that"). Purchase-centered recommendations are most valuable because they reveal actual buying behavior. Unprompted recommendations from peers carry more weight than any review or testimonial.
- **QUESTION** — What they're actively seeking to learn. Includes how-to questions (skill gaps), recommendation requests (active shopping), troubleshooting (failure points), best practices (improvement desire), and comparison questions (decision criteria). Don't just track questions — note which get detailed answers (topics that matter to the community) vs. which get ignored (topics that don't).
- **JARGON** — The exact language the audience uses in natural conversation. Specialty words outsiders don't understand, insider lingo that looks familiar but means something different in context, emotional intensifiers that reveal pain intensity. Capture exact phrases, not paraphrases. Build a running glossary — when you later create content or messaging for this audience, pull directly from this glossary so it sounds native, not like marketing.

**Staleness Threshold:** 2 years — user behavior and preferences shift with market conditions

**What to Analyze:**
- Digital watering holes — where does this audience gather? Reddit, LinkedIn, Discord, industry forums, newsletters, conferences. Map platform-by-platform. Different platforms reveal different facets: Reddit shows raw frustration, LinkedIn shows professional pain points, Discord shows enthusiast behavior, forums show technical depth, Twitter/X shows breaking sentiment.
- Pains and struggles — what do they complain about repeatedly? Look for frequency (how often the same issue appears), emotional intensity (language like "terrified," "desperate," "fed up" vs. "mildly annoying"), and behavioral signals (repeated questions, workaround descriptions, tool-switching patterns). Trust behavior over stated severity.
- Delights and loyalty drivers — what creates genuine satisfaction? Why do people stick with solutions despite alternatives? What unexpected benefits do they describe? Understanding loyalty drivers reveals what the audience truly values, which may be completely different from what vendors think they value.
- Recommendations and trust patterns — who and what do they recommend unprompted? Which resources, tools, and people carry authority in this community? Track recommendation language separately because it reveals competitive landscapes — if everyone recommends three tools and none are yours, you have a positioning problem.
- Questions and knowledge gaps — what are they trying to learn? Which questions reveal decision-making moments (high-intent)? Which reveal skill gaps (education opportunity)? Which reveal product failure points (differentiation opportunity)?
- Jargon and communication patterns — how do they talk about their world? A company might position as "user-friendly" but if the audience says they need tools that "don't fight you" or "get out of the way," that language difference matters. Exact phrases create resonance.
- Trend evaluation — for each pattern identified, score on 4 dimensions: Velocity (is conversation accelerating or flat?), Volume (one community or cross-platform?), Longevity (temporary event or structural shift?), Relevance (does it align with strategic goals?). Score each as High/Medium/Low. Patterns scoring High on 3+ dimensions deserve attention.
- Competitive positioning — what are competitors doing about the trends you've identified? Where are the gaps in market coverage? What aspects are competitors not addressing? What complaints persist even among users of existing solutions?

**Source Credibility Hierarchy:**
- Community posts where people describe experiences in their own words (Reddit, forums, Discord, Twitter/X) → highest credibility. Unprompted, authentic, no incentive to perform.
- User reviews on existing tools (G2, Capterra, Product Hunt, app stores) → high for understanding what works and what doesn't about current solutions
- Community polls and informal surveys with visible participation (>50 responses) → high for sentiment direction, moderate for statistical precision
- Professional discussions (LinkedIn, industry Slack groups, conference talks) → high for professional pain points, moderate for authenticity (more curated than anonymous platforms)
- Newsletter and blog content from community insiders (not vendors) → moderate for trend identification, reflects individual author's perspective
- Industry reports (Gartner, Forrester, etc.) → moderate for macro framing, low for niche-specific insight because they rarely go deep enough
- Competitor content and marketing → low for actual user experience, useful for mapping what competitors claim and how they position
- AI-generated summaries without sources → not a source, do not use

**Boundaries:**
- This is market intelligence, not product design or content creation. The output is a strategic intelligence report with prioritized findings. Do not produce product specs, content calendars, go-to-market plans, or wireframes.
- Capture what people actually say, not what you think they mean. Every tagged observation must include the exact quote or close paraphrase with source link. Do not interpret community sentiment without evidence.
- Distinguish painkillers from vitamins. Tag every PAIN finding as 🔥 Painkiller or 💊 Vitamin based on emotional intensity and solution-seeking behavior. Do not present vitamins as urgent opportunities. A vivid complaint is not the same as a problem someone would pay to solve.
- The tier system is about focus, not importance. Tier 4 (ignore) is as valuable as Tier 1 (primary focus). Knowing what not to pursue enables focus on what matters. Be explicit about what to ignore and why.

**Phase Pattern:** 6-8 phases, adapted by the plan generator based on audience complexity and community landscape. Typical structure:
- Phase 1: Audience definition and watering hole mapping (who are they, where do they gather, platform-by-platform inventory with notes on what each platform reveals)
- Phase 2: Multi-source observation and labeling (systematic reading across communities, tagging every observation as PAIN/DELIGHT/RECOMMENDATION/QUESTION/JARGON, capturing exact quotes with source links)
- Phase 3: Pain analysis and painkiller/vitamin classification (group pains by theme, classify each as painkiller or vitamin, identify workaround patterns that signal unmet demand)
- Phase 4: Trend evaluation and pattern scoring (apply Velocity/Volume/Longevity/Relevance scoring to each identified pattern, separate signal from noise, identify which patterns are accelerating)
- Phase 5: Competitive intelligence and positioning gaps (what competitors are doing about the trends you've found, where gaps exist, what angles are unclaimed, what complaints persist despite existing solutions)
- Phase 6: Strategic prioritization and tier assignment (assign Tier 1-4 to each finding: Tier 1 = primary focus 80% resources, Tier 2 = secondary 15%, Tier 3 = experimental 5%, Tier 4 = actively ignore 0%)
- Phase 7: Synthesis — strategic intelligence report with tiered findings, trend scorecards, competitive gap map, audience language glossary, and recommended actions per tier

Collapse phases 2 and 3 if the audience is concentrated in few communities. Expand phase 2 into multiple phases if the audience spans many distinct platforms with different cultures (e.g., separate phases for Reddit-heavy observation vs. LinkedIn/professional observation).

**Success Criteria:**
1. At least 5 community platforms have been systematically observed with tagged observations from each
2. Every observation is tagged with one of the 5 safari labels (PAIN/DELIGHT/RECOMMENDATION/QUESTION/JARGON) and includes an exact quote or close paraphrase with source link
3. Every PAIN finding is classified as 🔥 Painkiller or 💊 Vitamin with stated rationale for the classification
4. Every significant pattern is scored on all 4 trend dimensions (Velocity, Volume, Longevity, Relevance) with evidence supporting each score
5. Findings are assigned to Tiers 1-4 with explicit rationale for each tier placement, including Tier 4 items (what to ignore and why)
6. An audience language glossary is produced with exact phrases captured during observation
7. All outputs have been audited for unsupported claims — no trend is presented as validated without cross-platform evidence
